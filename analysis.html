Cocktail Clustering Analysis

Data-Driven Discovery of Natural Cocktail Neighborhoods

About This Analysis

This report uses k-means clustering to find natural groupings in 102 classic cocktails across four different vectorization strategies. Rather than imposing predetermined categories, we let the data reveal its own patterns.

Key Finding: The optimal number of clusters varies dramatically by strategy — from 3 clusters in pure flavor analysis to 4 in recipe grammar — suggesting that different lenses reveal different levels of complexity in the cocktail landscape.

Pure Flavor Profile (BLEND)

Figure 1: K-means found 3 optimal clusters (silhouette score: 0.598). Green = citrus sours, Bronze = whiskey territory, Silver = gin/bitter drinks.

The pure flavor approach produces the simplest clustering with just 3 natural groups. This makes intuitive sense: at the highest level, cocktails divide into fundamental flavor families that transcend specific ingredients or preparation methods.

Cluster 1: Citrus Sours (43 cocktails)
Core Members: Margarita, Daiquiri, Whiskey Sour, Gimlet, Mojito, Bee's Knees, Pisco Sour, Hemingway Daiquiri, Aviation, Clover Club, Sidecar, White Lady

The largest and most coherent cluster. These drinks are unified by their citrus-forward profile and sweet-sour balance. Notably, this cluster includes sours from all base spirits — a Whiskey Sour sits here with Daiquiris rather than with other whiskey drinks like Manhattans.

Cluster 2: Whiskey & Dark Spirits (34 cocktails)
Core Members: Manhattan, Old Fashioned, Boulevardier, Rob Roy, Sazerac, Black Manhattan, Vieux Carré, Brooklyn, Toronto, Rusty Nail

Spirit-forward drinks dominated by whiskey's oak, grain, and spice notes, often combined with sweet vermouth or other modifiers. Interestingly, Boulevardier clusters here despite being structurally a Negroni — flavor wins over structure in this view.

Cluster 3: Gin & Bitter Aperitivo (25 cocktails)
Core Members: Martini, Negroni, Martinez, Bijou, Hanky Panky, Americano, Alaska, Vesper, Fifty-Fifty Martini

A mixed cluster combining gin's juniper-forward cocktails with bitter Italian aperitivos. The presence of both Negroni and Martini in the same cluster suggests these seemingly different drinks share more flavor DNA than expected — likely through the herbal complexity of vermouth.

Notable Observations

  • Trinidad Sour appears in Cluster 3 — its Angostura base creates such a unique profile that it lands with the complex herbal drinks despite being structurally a sour.
  • Boulevardier splits from Negroni — same structure, but bourbon's flavor pulls it into whiskey territory (Cluster 2) while Negroni stays with gin drinks (Cluster 3).
  • The citrus boundary is absolute — once a drink has significant citrus, it gets pulled into Cluster 1 regardless of other characteristics.

Recipe Grammar (ROLE-SLOT)

Figure 2: K-means found 4 optimal clusters (silhouette score: 0.804). The highest silhouette score suggests structural patterns are more distinct than flavor patterns.

When we analyze cocktails by their grammatical structure — how ingredients fill functional roles — we get 4 distinct clusters with the highest silhouette score (0.804) of any strategy. This suggests that cocktail construction follows clearer rules than flavor composition.

Cluster 1: Complex Stirred Drinks (23 cocktails)
Core Members: Martinez, Bijou, Vieux Carré, Negroni, Boulevardier, Brooklyn, Little Italy, Tipperary, Hanky Panky

Multi-component stirred cocktails with complex modifier and accent roles. These drinks typically have 3+ ingredients beyond the base spirit, creating layered flavor profiles through careful role assignment.

Cluster 2: Sour Templates (52 cocktails)
Core Members: Daiquiri, Margarita, Whiskey Sour, Last Word, Paper Plane, Aviation, Bee's Knees, Clover Club, Sidecar, Cosmopolitan

The largest cluster encompasses all drinks following the base + citrus + sweetener grammar. This includes both simple sours and equal-parts drinks like Last Word, showing that the presence of citrus in a structural role is the defining characteristic.

Cluster 3: Two-Part Stirred Classics (19 cocktails)
Core Members: Martini, Manhattan, Rob Roy, Black Manhattan, Rusty Nail, Toronto, Alaska, Monte Carlo

Classic stirred drinks with primarily two components: base + modifier. These are the most traditional templates, often with just bitters as seasoning. The structural simplicity creates a tight, well-defined cluster.

Cluster 4: Old Fashioned Family (8 cocktails)
All Members: Old Fashioned, Sazerac, Oaxacan Old Fashioned, Rum Old Fashioned, Mint Julep, Scotch Old Fashioned, Irish Old Fashioned, Tequila Old Fashioned

The smallest but most coherent cluster — drinks built directly in the glass with minimal modification of the base spirit. The grammar is so specific (base + sweetener + bitters) that it forms its own category regardless of which spirit is used.

Structural Insights

  • Equal-parts drinks split — Last Word goes with sours (has citrus), while Negroni goes with complex stirred drinks (no citrus).
  • The Old Fashioned template is universal — 8 different base spirits, but the grammar is so distinctive they form their own cluster.
  • 52% of cocktails are sours — structurally, this is the dominant template in classic cocktail making.

Intensity-Weighted (PERCEPTUAL)

Figure 3: K-means found 3 optimal clusters (silhouette score: 0.845). The highest score overall — intensity creates the clearest boundaries.

When we weight ingredients by their perceptual intensity, we get the clearest clustering of all strategies (silhouette: 0.845). Only 3 clusters emerge, but they're exceptionally well-defined, suggesting that human perception of cocktails may be dominated by intensity peaks rather than volume-weighted averages.

Cluster 1: Gin & Intense Herbal (23 cocktails)
Core Members: Martini, Negroni, Bijou, Hanky Panky, Martinez, Alaska, Last Word (when Chartreuse dominates), Vesper

Drinks where gin's juniper intensity or powerful herbal liqueurs (Chartreuse, Fernet) define the experience. Even when these ingredients are used sparingly, their intensity pulls drinks into this cluster.

Cluster 2: Citrus & Light (52 cocktails)
Core Members: Margarita, Daiquiri, Mojito, Cosmopolitan, French 75, Paloma, Aperol Spritz, Bee's Knees

The citrus cluster remains massive and coherent. In perceptual terms, once citrus is present, it tends to dominate the sensory experience, overwhelming subtler flavor notes.

Cluster 3: Dark & Smoky Spirits (27 cocktails)
Core Members: Manhattan, Old Fashioned, Penicillin, Boulevardier, Oaxacan Old Fashioned, Sazerac, Rob Roy, Vieux Carré

Whiskey-forward drinks plus anything with smoky intensity. Penicillin appears here due to its Islay float — just 7.5ml defines the entire drink's perceptual category.

Intensity Effects

Example: Penicillin's Classification
60ml blended Scotch (smoke=0.1) + 7.5ml Islay float (smoke=0.9)
Standard weighting: (60×0.1 + 7.5×0.9)/67.5 = 0.19 smoke
Intensity weighting: 0.4×0.9 + 0.6×0.19 = 0.47 smoke
Result: 2.5× higher smoke value → clusters with whiskey, not citrus sours
  • Chartreuse creates its own gravity — drinks with even small amounts cluster together regardless of other ingredients.
  • Absinthe rinses matter — Sazerac's rinse is enough to shift its perceptual profile significantly.
  • The clearest boundaries yet — 0.845 silhouette score suggests this might best match how humans actually experience cocktails.

Balanced Mix (50% Structure + 50% Flavor)

Figure 4: K-means found 3 optimal clusters (silhouette score: 0.777). A transitional view showing drinks caught between structural and flavor categories.

At the midpoint between structure and flavor, we see cocktails in transition. The clustering is less distinct (silhouette: 0.777) but reveals which drinks truly straddle category boundaries.

Cluster 1: Stirred Classics (33 cocktails)
Core Members: Martini, Manhattan, Martinez, Bijou, Vieux Carré, Black Manhattan, Rob Roy, Tipperary

Traditional stirred drinks maintain their coherence even when structure and flavor are equally weighted. These are the most stable cocktails across all strategies.

Cluster 2: Built & Bitter (29 cocktails)
Core Members: Old Fashioned, Negroni, Americano, Penicillin, Mezcal Negroni, Oaxacan Old Fashioned, Mint Julep

Drinks built in the glass or featuring prominent bitter elements. This mixed cluster shows how preparation method begins to override flavor at this weighting.

Cluster 3: Shaken Sours (40 cocktails)
Core Members: Daiquiri, Margarita, Last Word, Paper Plane, Bee's Knees, Aviation, Whiskey Sour, Sidecar

Shaken drinks with citrus maintain their identity. The physical preparation method (shaking) combined with citrus presence creates a strong categorical boundary.

Boundary Dwellers

At 50/50 weighting, certain cocktails reveal their dual nature:

  • Boulevardier — Pulled between Cluster 1 (whiskey stirred) and Cluster 2 (Negroni family)
  • Hanky Panky — Torn between Martini structure and Fernet intensity
  • Penicillin — Can't decide if it's a shaken sour or a smoky Old Fashioned

Key Findings

The Data Tells a Different Story

K-means clustering revealed simpler, more fundamental patterns than expected:

  • Fewer clusters are optimal — 3-4 natural groups, not the 6+ often discussed in cocktail books
  • Structure creates clearer boundaries than flavor — 0.804 silhouette for ROLE-SLOT vs 0.598 for BLEND
  • Intensity-weighting produces the clearest clustering — 0.845 silhouette suggests this matches human perception
  • Citrus is a primary organizing principle — appears as a major cluster in every strategy

Recommendations for Interface Design

Based on this k-means analysis, the interface should be simplified to highlight the natural clustering patterns found in the data:

1. Show Fewer, Clearer Clusters

Instead of trying to identify 6+ clusters, acknowledge that cocktails naturally form 3-4 major groups. Users can understand "citrus drinks," "whiskey drinks," and "gin/bitter drinks" more easily than dozens of micro-categories.

2. Prioritize Intensity-Weighted View

With the highest silhouette score (0.845), the PERCEPTUAL strategy appears to best match how humans actually experience cocktails. This should be the default or primary view.

3. Highlight Boundary Dwellers

Cocktails like Boulevardier, Penicillin, and Last Word that move between clusters are the most interesting. They deserve special notation as "crossover" drinks that bridge categories.

4. Use Radio Buttons, Not Sliders

The data shows distinct clustering at strategy endpoints but confusion at midpoints. Four radio buttons (BLEND, ROLE-SLOT, PERCEPTUAL, 50/50) would be clearer than 21 slider positions.

Conclusion

This data-driven analysis reveals that cocktail similarity is simpler than often portrayed. Rather than dozens of nuanced categories, cocktails naturally organize into 3-4 major clusters that shift based on whether we prioritize flavor, structure, or perceptual intensity.

The most interesting finding is that intensity-weighted perception produces the clearest clustering, suggesting that our experience of cocktails is dominated by the most intense flavors present, not by volume-weighted averages. A tiny amount of Chartreuse, Fernet, or smoky Islay can completely redefine a drink's category.

For interface design, this suggests focusing on these natural groupings rather than forcing artificial categories. Let users explore the genuine patterns in the data — including the fascinating boundary cases that refuse to stay in one neighborhood.
